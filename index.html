Designer notes (v0.6 — generation rows + expanded charts)

  • Fungus mechanic: Students cross plants first, then introduce fungus. Once introduced, the button locks permanently — fungus cannot be removed. This models real-world irreversibility of fungal contamination.
  • Generation rows: Each cross creates a labeled row (G1, G2, G3…) in the offspring grid with a light border. This helps students correlate generations between the grid and the line graph x-axis.
  • Fungus marker (both views): A blue dashed line appears in BOTH the offspring grid (horizontal divider) and the line graph (vertical marker) at the generation when fungus was introduced. This reinforces the connection between the two visualizations.
  • Vertical marker on line graph: A blue dashed line labeled "Fungus introduced" appears at the generation when the button was clicked. Pre-fungus data points show 100% healthy (students can't observe resistance). Post-fungus data points show actual resistance.
  • Workflow: (1) Select parents → (2) Cross several times (all plants look healthy) → (3) Click "Introduce Fungus" (locks, marker appears, existing plants reveal phenotype) → (4) Keep crossing to see how resistance changes over more generations.
  • Pie chart + grid: Before fungus, all plants appear green and pie shows 100% healthy. After fungus, non-resistant plants turn brown/wilted and pie shows real percentages.
  • Reset Trial: Clears everything including the fungus state, so students can start a fresh experiment in that tab.

Questions for the educator panel

  1. Offspring sample size & randomness: Each cross currently produces 5–20 random offspring. Small samples mean students might get a 50/50 cross that happens to come out 3 resistant / 9 not-resistant, which could lead to incorrect but reasonable conclusions. Is that variability pedagogically valuable (teaching about sample size), or should the simulation nudge results closer to expected Mendelian ratios to keep the assessment fair?
  2. Fungus reversibility: The current design makes introducing fungus a one-way permanent action per trial. Should students be allowed to turn fungus off within a trial after it has already been introduced? Is it important that students can choose when to introduce fungus (after 1 cross, after 3, etc.), or should the simulation prompt them at a fixed point?
  3. Pre-fungus plant appearance: Right now, pre-fungus plants all look identically healthy — students literally cannot tell resistant from non-resistant. Is that level of ambiguity appropriate for grades 6–8, or should there be some subtle visual hint (like slightly different leaf shapes for wild vs. Cavendish offspring) so students have something to observe and discuss even before introducing the pathogen?
  4. Line graph metric & marker label: The "Fungus Resistance over Generations" chart tracks cumulative resistance percentage. Is "cumulative across all offspring so far" the right metric, or should each generation point reflect only the offspring from that specific cross? Cumulative data smooths out noise but obscures generational trends. Also, is the "Fungus introduced" vertical marker label clear enough, or should it include a brief explanation like "Plants now exposed to fungus"?
  5. Vocabulary level: Are terms like "phenotype" and "resistance" appropriate for the target grade band, or should the interface use simplified language like "what the plant looks like" and "ability to fight off disease"?
  6. Time on task: How long will students typically have with the simulation during the assessment?
